Unmoot - Class Participation Platform

2025

University of Queensland | Product Designer

Unmoot, was developed as part of the Design Computing Studio 3: Proposal course at the University of Queensland. Our team set out to explore the barriers to class participation among IT students and design an inclusive solution to encourage engagement. My role primarily involved user research, synthesizing insights, and leading the design of the prototype in Figma. Over the course of the semester, I collaborated with my teammates to conduct interviews, analyze findings, and iterate on the solution.

THE CHALLENGE

Why was class participation such a problem worth solving?

Class participation is a critical element of active learning, yet many students find it difficult to contribute in classroom discussions. Our research revealed a range of barriers including fear of judgement, peer pressure, large class sizes, language difficulties, and teaching styles. These challenges limited both international and domestic students, preventing meaningful contributions in tutorials and lectures. Our goal was to design a solution that would reduce these barriers and create a more inclusive learning environment for all students.

THE RESEARCH

How did we uncover the real barriers to participation?

We began by focusing on accent-related barriers to participation, assuming this was the primary issue affecting international students. However, after feedback and early interviews, we realized this framing was too narrow. We pivoted to a broader exploration of classroom participation challenges as a whole. To investigate, we conducted a literature review, held semi-structured interviews with students and lecturers, and coded the transcripts on Miro to extract themes.

From this process, we identified multiple factors to which we narrowed down to 4

Peer influence and class size

Teaching style and faculty behavior

Language and communication barriers

Personal factors such as anxiety and fear of being wrong

DEFINING THE PROBLEM

How might we reduce barriers to classroom participation and create inclusive ways for students to engage in discussions?

IDEATION AND CONCEPT DEVELOPMENT

What ideas did we explore, and how did we choose a direction?

During ideation, we explored various ways to support participation without increasing pressure on students. Early concepts included tools for anonymous contributions, comprehension aids, and peer-driven collaboration. Through feedback sessions and low-fidelity prototypes, we refined our direction toward table-based collaboration and real-time comprehension support. This ensured that students could engage in smaller, safer group formats while still contributing to the larger class discussion.

We followed an iterative design process, moving from sketches and wireframes to mid-fidelity prototypes and eventually a semi-high-fidelity prototype in Figma. My focus was on creating a clear, minimal interface that encouraged contribution without distractions. Accessibility was central to our design, leading us to include multimodal inputs to ensure every student could participate in ways that felt comfortable. Regular feedback from peers and lecturers ensured the system aligned with real teaching and learning practices.

Unmoot

Our final concept, Unmoot, is a digital-physical hybrid platform that supports inclusive, low-pressure class participation. The system consists of three screens: a table-based discussion screen, a student device interface, and a lecturer dashboard.

TABLE SCREEN

Encouraging Group Participation

The Table Screen was designed to shift the pressure away from individuals and encourage collective participation. By focusing on group discussions, students feel less anxious about being "put on the spot" and more comfortable sharing ideas with their peers. This shared hub acts as a natural icebreaker, helping students build connections and confidence before contributing to the wider class discussion. The format also allows quieter students to be heard, as their voices are supported within a group submission rather than judged in isolation.

STUDENT SCREEN

How do students interact with the platform?

The Student Screen was designed to provide every learner with a personal space for safe, inclusive engagement. Many students hesitate to speak out loud, but with this interface, they can choose from multiple ways of contributing — whether typing, drawing, speaking, or even uploading an image. This flexibility gives students ownership over how they communicate and ensures language barriers don't limit participation. The screen also includes a live transcript of lectures, which is particularly helpful for international students or those who struggle with note-taking. To further support comprehension, the AI-powered simplification tool allows students to revisit complex ideas in plain language, while the comprehension slider lets them indicate whether they're following along, giving lecturers instant feedback they can act on.

LECTURER SCREEN

How do lecturers monitor and facilitate discussions?

The Lecturer Dashboard brings everything together by giving instructors real-time visibility into student engagement. Instead of relying on a handful of raised hands, lecturers can now see patterns across the entire class, identifying which groups are struggling and where comprehension drops. The dashboard makes it easy to launch quick Live Pulse tasks, either for groups or individuals, which help check understanding in the moment without interrupting the flow of teaching. It also surfaces student questions in one consolidated view, reducing the chance that quieter students are overlooked. Importantly, the dashboard is designed to reduce — not increase — lecturer workload by automating participation tracking and highlighting insights that would otherwise be invisible.

ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS

What risks did we need to address while designing?

We carefully considered ethical implications throughout the design. Concerns included data privacy and student consent, AI-generated simplifications potentially misrepresenting content, accessibility for students without reliable devices, and the risk of weakening teacher–student relationships. Our approach was to design a system that supported lecturers rather than replacing them, ensuring their autonomy remained central.

USER TESTING

Did the solution actually work for students and lecturers?

To evaluate usability, we conducted a System Usability Scale (SUS) survey with 20 students while they interacted with the prototype. Unmoot scored 87, reflecting excellent usability. Students praised the badge system and multimodal input options, while lecturers appreciated the potential for real-time insights. However, lecturers also raised concerns about added workload and questioned how the system would scale across different class formats.

REFLECTION

What did I learn from this project?

This project taught me the importance of flexibility in design research. Our pivot from accent-specific issues to broader participation barriers reinforced the need to challenge assumptions and remain adaptable. I learned the value of designing for multiple stakeholders and balancing user needs with practical feasibility.

Personally, I developed stronger skills in qualitative research, thematic analysis, and turning insights into actionable design features. Moving forward, I see opportunities to test Unmoot in real classrooms, refine the AI-driven features, and integrate with institutional learning systems to create lasting impact.
